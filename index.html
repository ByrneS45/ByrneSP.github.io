I’m a PhD candidate in Experimental Nuclear Physics at the University of Massachusetts Lowell in Dr. Andrew Rogers’ group. My work uses gamma-ray spectroscopy and coincidence techniques to extract nuclear structure information and constrain astrophysical reaction rates relevant to nucleosynthesis (including supernova-relevant temperatures).

I build analysis workflows in C++ (ROOT) and Python for detector calibration, peak fitting, γ–γ coincidence spectra, and uncertainty propagation. I also develop numerical tools to calculate non-resonant and resonant thermonuclear reaction rates (narrow resonance and broader contributions), including penetrability and width calculations.

My research places a strong emphasis on nuclear data: translating experimental observables into reaction and decay information, validating results against evaluated datasets (e.g., ENSDF/NNDC), and developing reproducible analysis pipelines. I am particularly interested in modern computational approaches—including automation and emerging machine-learning tools—to accelerate nuclear data analysis and improve accessibility for the broader community.

Explosive Nucleosynthesis & the Origin of the Elements

In the final moments of massive stars—and in thermonuclear white-dwarf explosions—temperatures reach billions of kelvin. Nuclear reaction timescales collapse from millions of years to seconds, driving explosive nucleosynthesis: rapid burning (O/Ne/Si), α-rich freezeout, and neutron-capture pathways that forge nuclei from the iron group to the actinides.

These events eject freshly synthesized material into the interstellar medium, enriching future generations of stars and planets. The natural abundances we measure today are a fossil record of these processes: the Big Bang sets H/He, stellar fusion builds up to the iron peak, and explosive events shape the heavy-element tail.

Why abundances look the way they do

The iron peak reflects nuclear binding-energy stability and efficient production in supernova burning. The odd-even effect and heavy-element “tail” encode neutron-capture histories and explosive conditions.

Supernovae: element factories

Shock heating triggers explosive burning, while expanding ejecta seed the galaxy with newly made nuclei—material that later becomes stars, planets, and ultimately us.

What remnants reveal

Multi-wavelength images map different temperatures and elements in the debris, linking observed structures to explosion dynamics and nucleosynthesis yields.

Rapid neutron capture

In extremely neutron-rich conditions, nuclei climb to high mass numbers via fast captures before β-decays return them toward stability—building roughly half of the elements heavier than iron.
