üåü Explosive Nucleosynthesis, Supernovae, and the Origin of the Elements

Explosive nucleosynthesis is the rapid production of new atomic nuclei during extreme astrophysical events. When temperatures reach billions of kelvin, reaction timescales collapse from millions of years to seconds‚Äîdriving capture processes and photodisintegration flows that shape the elemental and isotopic patterns we observe today.

An insight into what I study

Where the elements come from

The Solar System‚Äôs composition reflects a mixture of Big Bang nucleosynthesis, long-lived stellar fusion, and explosive events that synthesize and eject new material into the galaxy, the later being where most of my efforts have been in understanding.

The constant dance between graventational and nuclear forces

Stellar burning is complex and evolves through many stages of a star‚Äôs lifetime. Within different regions of a star, a wide variety of thermodynamic environments exist, each enabling the production of different elements. These nuclei are synthesized through sequences of nuclear reactions known as reaction pathways. The nucleosynthesis occurring in these environments not only builds the elements but also provides the energy that counteracts gravitational collapse and sustains the star in hydrostatic equilibrium. Pathways on the chart of nuclides

Which path dominates depends on temperature, density, and the availability of neutrons vs protons: captures compete with Œ≤-decays, photodisintegration, and neutrino interactions. Most of my work lies on the proton rich side of stability, meaning above the stable (black) nuclei. The rp process and vp process prodominatly.

Neutron capture & heavy elements

The r-process proceeds far from stability under extreme neutron richness, then Œ≤-decays back toward stability‚Äî generating much of the heavy-element inventory. This process occurs in neutron star mergers, seen through gravitational waves. There is also unique conditions in Supernovae that this process may occur. This process is where many of the heavier elements are thought to be created and can see the pathway in dark purple on the lower portion of th chart of nuclides in the figure to the right.

Proton-rich pathways: p, rp, and ŒΩp

p-process (Œ≥-process): produces certain proton-rich stable isotopes (‚Äúp-nuclei‚Äù) mainly through photodisintegration reactions ((Œ≥,n), (Œ≥,p), (Œ≥,Œ±)) on pre-existing heavy seeds in hot, explosive layers.

rp-process: rapid proton captures and Œ≤+ decays in hot, H-rich environments (classically X-ray bursts), pushing material along the proton-rich side until ‚Äúwaiting points‚Äù slow the flow.

ŒΩp-process: in proton-rich supernova outflows, neutrino interactions supply a small neutron density that enables (n,p) reactions to bypass rp waiting points, extending synthesis to heavier nuclei.

Neutron-capture pathways: s, i, and r

s-process: ‚Äúslow‚Äù neutron captures (relative to Œ≤-decay) in quiescent stellar burning; the path stays near the valley of stability.

i-process: ‚Äúintermediate‚Äù neutron flux between s and r, producing distinctive abundance patterns that can explain some chemically peculiar stars.

r-process: ‚Äúrapid‚Äù neutron captures in very neutron-rich conditions, driving far from stability and then Œ≤-decaying back.

Pathway cheat sheet: s = slow n-capture i = intermediate n-capture r = rapid n-capture rp = rapid p-capture ŒΩp = neutrino-aided proton-rich flow p/Œ≥ = photodisintegration p-nuclei
