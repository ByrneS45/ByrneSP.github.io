I’m a PhD candidate in Experimental Nuclear Physics at the University of Massachusetts Lowell in Dr. Andrew Rogers’ group. My work uses gamma-ray spectroscopy and coincidence techniques to extract nuclear structure information and constrain astrophysical reaction rates relevant to nucleosynthesis (including supernova-relevant temperatures).

I build analysis workflows in C++ (ROOT) and Python for detector calibration, peak fitting, γ–γ coincidence spectra, and uncertainty propagation. I also develop numerical tools to calculate non-resonant and resonant thermonuclear reaction rates (narrow resonance and broader contributions), including penetrability and width calculations.

My research places a strong emphasis on nuclear data: translating experimental observables into reaction and decay information, validating results against evaluated datasets (e.g., ENSDF/NNDC), and developing reproducible analysis pipelines. I am particularly interested in modern computational approaches—including automation and emerging machine-learning tools—to accelerate nuclear data analysis and improve accessibility for the broader community.

Explosive Nucleosynthesis & the Origin of the Elements

In the final moments of massive stars—and in thermonuclear white-dwarf explosions—temperatures reach billions of kelvin. Nuclear reaction timescales collapse from millions of years to seconds, driving explosive nucleosynthesis: rapid burning (O/Ne/Si), α-rich freezeout, and neutron- and proton-capture pathways that shape the elemental and isotopic patterns we observe today.

These events eject freshly synthesized material into the interstellar medium, enriching future generations of stars and planets. The natural abundances in the Solar System are a “fossil record”: Big Bang nucleosynthesis sets H/He, stellar fusion builds up to the iron peak, and explosive events populate much of the heavy-element inventory.

Why abundances look the way they do

The iron peak reflects nuclear binding-energy stability and efficient production in explosive burning. Odd-even structure and the heavy-element tail encode capture histories and the thermodynamic conditions of the ejecta.

Supernovae: element factories

Shock heating triggers explosive burning, while expanding ejecta seed the galaxy with newly made nuclei—material that later becomes stars, planets, and ultimately us.

What remnants reveal

Multi-wavelength images map different temperatures and elements in the debris, linking observed structures to explosion dynamics and nucleosynthesis yields.

Rapid neutron capture (r-process)

In extremely neutron-rich conditions, nuclei undergo fast captures before β-decays return them toward stability—building much of the material heavier than iron.

“Origin map” of the periodic table

A compact way to visualize how different sites contribute: Big Bang, stellar fusion, explosive massive stars, and long-lived low-mass stars (plus cosmic-ray spallation for light elements like Li–Be–B).

Slow vs rapid capture

The s-process follows the valley of stability (captures are slow compared to β-decays), while the r-process runs far to the neutron-rich side (captures are fast), then decays back toward stability.

Abundances by nuclide

Isotopic abundances highlight what’s common (H, He, O, Fe) and what’s rare—often controlled by “bottlenecks” in reaction flows and by the availability of neutrons/protons in explosive environments.

Proton-rich pathways (p / rp / νp)

rp-process: rapid proton captures and β+ decays in hot, H-rich environments (classically X-ray bursts), pushing along the proton-rich side until “waiting points” slow the flow.

νp-process (vp): in proton-rich supernova outflows, neutrino interactions can supply free neutrons that enable (n,p) reactions to bypass rp waiting points, extending synthesis to heavier nuclei.

p-process (γ-process): production of certain proton-rich stable isotopes (“p-nuclei”) mainly via photodisintegration ((γ,n), (γ,p), (γ,α)) on pre-existing heavy seeds in hot explosive layers.

Neutron-capture pathways (s / i / r)

s-process: “slow” captures (relative to β-decay) in quiescent stellar burning; tends to follow stable isotopes.

i-process: “intermediate” neutron flux between s and r, producing distinctive abundance patterns that can explain some observed stellar compositions; conceptually sits between the two extremes in capture rate.

r-process: “rapid” captures in very neutron-rich conditions, driving far from stability and then β-decaying back.

Pathway cheat sheet: s = slow n-capture i = intermediate n-capture r = rapid n-capture rp = rapid p-capture νp (vp) = neutrino-aided proton-rich flow p (γ) = photodisintegration-driven p-nuclei
